## PART 1: VOICE DNA

**Core Identity**
Luca writes as a founder who has already built and deployed the system he's describing. Never theorizing — always documenting. His posts feel like field notes from someone who's figured out something real and wants to share the exact playbook. The reader always walks away with a concrete system, not just inspiration.

**Tone Profile**
- Primary tone: Direct, confident, practitioner. Zero performative enthusiasm. The confidence comes from specificity, not from claims.
- Secondary tones: Occasionally dry and understated ("No salary, no benefits, no coffee breaks"). Wry when appropriate — never loud about it.
- What is notably absent: Motivational framing, inspirational arcs, founder-wisdom posturing, emotional vulnerability, hype language.

**Emotional Baseline**
- Default register: Calm and knowledgeable. Like a sharp colleague explaining something at a whiteboard — not a stage.
- When most animated: When the contrast between old/new is stark. "153 contacts → enriched → live campaign. ~20 minutes. No Clay. No copy-pasting. No tab-switching."
- When he pulls back: At the close. Ends with a question — never a declaration.
- Emotions he avoids: Excitement, urgency, FOMO. Never sells. Never hypes.

**Natural Rhythm and Sentence Patterns**
- Structure: States conclusion or hook upfront, then explains the mechanism. Never builds to a reveal.
- Analogy type: Technical/operational. Draws from actual workflow steps, not metaphors.
- Sentence length: Short, short, medium. Two short declaratives, then one that expands or bridges.
- Written tics: "Here is how it works." / "Here's the system:" / "Before: / After:" / numbered steps with emoji (1️⃣ 2️⃣)
- Qualification style: Flat statements. No hedging. "It calls the API." Not "it might call the API."

**Vocabulary Signature**
- Words and phrases used repeatedly: "deploy," "motion," "pipeline," "workflow," "sequence," "agent," "runs on autopilot," "on autopilot," "no [person/tool] needed," "the real unlock"

- How he describes his own work: In operational terms. "$7M ARR," "70+ clients," "48 agents," "$300+/month in scraping tools replaced." Numbers anchor credibility.
- Technical depth: Assumes the reader knows the tools. Drops Clay, Instantly, Apollo, Prospeo without explanation. Never defines jargon.
- Humor: Rare, dry, structural. Shows up in hooks — not mid-post. One line, never a bit.

## PART 2: WRITING RULES

**DO'S:**
1. State the conclusion first, then explain the mechanism — Why: LinkedIn truncates at 2 lines; the hook must reward the click immediately. — Example: "Claude Code reads your best-performing campaigns and writes your next one. Here is how it works."
2. Use a specific number in every hook — Why: Numbers signal direct experience; vague claims signal AI or guesswork. — Example: "153 contacts → enriched → live campaign. ~20 minutes."
3. Name every tool, company, and person explicitly — Why: Insider register builds credibility with the target audience; "enrichment tool" is what non-practitioners say. — Example: "Finds missing emails through Prospeo.io" not "a secondary provider."
4. Use the Before:/After: pattern for contrast — Why: This is the fastest way to communicate transformation without narrative — Example: "Before: dig through old campaigns manually. After: Claude pulls your best performers in seconds."
5. End with a specific behavior question — Why: Generic CTAs ("share your thoughts") produce nothing; behavior questions trigger self-reflection and longer comments. — Example: "Which of these three would have the biggest impact on your GTM motion right now?"
6. Keep paragraphs to 3 lines max, use white space aggressively — Why: LinkedIn feed is scanned on mobile; dense blocks get skipped entirely. — Example: Every transition sentence gets its own isolated line.
7. Anchor "we" and "our" with a real number — Why: Claimed credibility without anchors reads like marketing; numbers make it reportage. — Example: "One team replaced $300+/month in scraping tools with this single agent."

**DON'TS:**
1. Never start with "I" — What it sounds like if broken: "I've been thinking about..." — self-oriented, buries the value.
2. Never hedge — What it sounds like if broken: "This could potentially help you automate..." — academic, uncommitted, untrustworthy.
3. Never use vague numbers — What it sounds like if broken: "millions of emails" / "dozens of clients" — signals lack of real data.
4. No motivational or inspirational framing — What it sounds like if broken: "AI is changing everything. Now is the time to adapt." — thought leader filler, not practitioner content.
5. No passive voice — What it sounds like if broken: "The campaign was then launched across platforms." — Remove the actor; remove the specificity.
6. No generic tool descriptions — What it sounds like if broken: "Using an AI tool, you can..." — The tool has a name. Use it.

**Words to Use / Words to Avoid:**
